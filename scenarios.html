About this course

This course synthesizes the learnings we have had from previous research into a concrete example of what is next in the education space. We acknowledge that these are unpredictable realities and are simply using forecasting tools to portray different scenarios based on a few key driving forces we chose.


References Here

Main Driving Forces



Cost of Higher Education

Value Placed on School Reputation



Why?

Expert interviews showed that many people come to Stanford Online High School for the name brand. Other research shows that reputation is becoming less important. The fact that this is uncertain yet has a huge impact on the future of work was interesting to us. The question of the cost of education is also interesting because different countries have different policies on education. Canada, for example, has tuition rates of around $7k which is far below the U.S. average. Many countries even have publicly funded schools. Given that we are at one of the most expensive universities, this seemed like an interesting spectrum to consider.

Go For Value

In this world, we have moved to a place where reputation of higher education degrees becomes less important. Companies value new employees with concrete and demonstrable skills rather than fancy degrees. Students are faced with high cost colleges that each have to now differentiate themselves with specific specialty areas like theater or computer science. Perhaps the number of schools in the U.S. reduced because of this. Students may try to seek the highest value colleges (those that offer the most skills for the least money) because there is no reputation-based incentive to pay more. This may have secondary effects such as decreasing the pressure of high school students during college application season. On the university side, students may have less of an incentive to donate to their university if there is less school pride.



Elite Schools

In this world, the cost of higher education continues to rise alongside their reputation. Students face an increased pressure to graduate with traditional 4-year degrees from highly-ranked schools. Schools may try to constantly update their brand and appeal by building impressive buildings, hosting conferences, sharing research work online, and visiting students at their high schools.



Open the Floodgates

In this world, education costs start to drop because of some major change in how we view accessible education and large endowments. However, employers have not changed and so the reputation and ranking of you school are increasingly important. These two changes allow thousands of students who once believed they may not be able to get a higher degree to apply to any school they want, creating a true meritocracy. Due to this influx however, the game may turn into a “who do you know” scenario in which only those with connections to admissions officers ever get their applications even read.



Free-For-All

In this world, education costs are driven down, making degrees more accessible. Furthermore, there is less institutional name-brand hierarchy which further levels the playing field, making elite institutions less relevant. This may look like a scattered collection of online resources in every field or more consolidated but varied online “schools” that pop-up based on demand. In this system, with most resources accessible to all, new students seeking to find work will need to differentiate themselves in new ways. The value of “side projects” or real life experience may be more values than education.



Final Thoughts

We define:
Reputation = perception of an organization or person based on history or tradition versus direct actions
Brand = a constantly changing perception of a group that they can change for better or worse.

In gauging the liklihood of these options we considered many variables. One signal we are seeing is the decreased need for a name brand school in applying for jobs or even achieving post-undergraduate degrees. This change in perception may lead to more accessible opportunities for students who may not be able to afford private institutions.
